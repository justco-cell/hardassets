## Reddit Post — r/Silverbugs or r/Wallstreetsilver

**Title:** Why Your Silver Bar Costs More Than Spot Price — A Cost Basis Reality Check

The spot price is a myth. When you buy a 100-oz silver bar at $30/oz spot, you're not paying $3,000 — you're paying ~$3,060 because of the premium.

I see this confusion constantly. Spot price is wholesale, industrial-bulk pricing. The moment you buy 1-oz coins or smaller bars, you're paying over spot. Here's what drives it:

**What premiums actually cover:**
- Minting and refining labor
- Shipping and insurance 
- Dealer overhead and profit margin
- Market volatility protection
- Rarity factors (for collectible coins)

**Real numbers from today:**
- 100-oz silver bar: 1-2% premium (~$30-60)
- 1-oz generic silver rounds: 3-4% premium (~$0.90-1.20)
- American Silver Eagles: 5-7% premium (~$1.50-2.10)

The bigger point: if you're tracking your hard asset portfolio (and you should be), your cost basis isn't spot price. It's spot + premium. Ignore this and you'll underestimate your true returns or overestimate losses.

Understanding precious metals premiums has been a game-changer for my investment tracking.

For years, I assumed spot price = what I'd pay. I was wrong. The premium—covering minting costs, shipping, insurance, and dealer margin—adds 3-8% to your actual cost basis. That's a material difference when you're managing thousands or tens of thousands in metals.

**Why this matters:**
🥇 Spot is wholesale; premiums are retail reality
💰 True cost basis affects tax reporting and ROI calculations  
📊 Different products have very different premiums (bars beat coins on cost)
📈 Market conditions move premiums; supply crunches spike them

The best investors understand premiums. They shop dealers, prefer bars when possible, and track costs accurately. If you own gold or silver, understanding this is foundational.
